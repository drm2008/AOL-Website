Section 4: Purpose of the Study

The Goal Statement

The Purpose of the Study is the core guiding pillar. Learn how to map a formal title directly into a targeted mission statement.

Defining the Goal

What is a Purpose Statement?

It is the primary guiding objective explaining why the research is happening and what specific, observable outcome is expected. It must explicitly declare:

  • The target variables.
  • The exact target population.
  • The intended real-world applications.
Tip: Keep it concise, focused, and free of unnecessary fluff. It guides all downstream variable controls.

Section 6: Research Hypotheses & Testing

Predictions and Empirical Verification

Hypotheses are testable predictions that guide experimental design. Below, explore both structural types and run a live simulated trial.

Anatomy of Null vs. Alternative Hypotheses

Null Hypothesis (H0) No Effect

"There is no statistically significant difference in test scores between students taught using the new teaching method and those taught using the traditional method."

Alternative Hypothesis (Ha) Has Effect

"Students taught using the new teaching method will achieve statistically significantly higher test scores compared to students taught using the traditional method."

Key Characteristics of a Sound Hypothesis

  • Testable: Must be formulated to allow empirical testing.
  • Relational: Proposes a concrete connection between variables.
  • Directional: Outlines the expected direction of change.
  • Grounded: Derived from existing theories or prior research.

Section 7: Variables & Data Types

Variables, Attributes, & Measurement Scales

Variables are the core objects of empirical study. Learn to classify their characteristics and measurement levels.

Variables & Attributes

A Variable is any entity that can take on different values (e.g., age, score). An Attribute is a specific value of that variable.

Variable Instance Student Grade Outcome
Attributes: Pass, Fail
Variable Instance Agreement Level
Attributes: Strongly Disagree, Disagree, Neutral, Agree, Strongly Agree
Independent vs. Dependent

The Independent Variable is manipulated or introduced (e.g., program or treatment). The Dependent Variable is the measured outcome affected by that manipulation.

Section 8: Unit of Analysis

What Exactly Are You Analyzing?

The Unit of Analysis is the major entity being studied. It dictates how data is compiled and compared.

Defining the Unit

The unit of analysis is defined by the level at which conclusions are drawn. It is often different from your unit of sampling. For example, you may sample individual students but analyze the entire classroom as a single group.

Active Unit Breakdown

Individuals as Unit of Analysis

If you are studying people and comparing their individual performances, each person is your primary unit of analysis. For example, comparing test scores of students across classes.

Scientific Contrast

Comparing individual test scores means the student is the unit. However, if you are studying classroom environmental atmospheres, the classroom itself becomes the unit of analysis since you measure it as a whole group.

No Relationship

Cross-Sectional Studies Single Instant

Cross-sectional studies occur at a single, isolated point in time. All variables are measured simultaneously.

Example Scenario:

"Surveying a group of students in one school to understand their preferences at a specific moment."

Longitudinal Studies Over Time

Longitudinal designs follow the same group over a period of time, measuring variables at multiple key points.

Repeated Measures

Fewer than 20 points (e.g., patient's BP trial measurements).

Time Series Design

20 or more measurements (e.g., daily market monitoring for a year).

Example Scenario:

"Tracking the development of the same group of individuals' career progression over several years."

Assumptions in Research

Assumptions are beliefs or conditions taken for granted and considered true, even though they may not be explicitly proven. They guide choices of methodology, sampling, and statistics.

Integrity check: Overstepping an assumption without proper scientific grounding can invalidate your entire research output.
